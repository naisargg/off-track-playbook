Sea-Freight Transition Simulator

F1 has committed to moving 50%+ of its broadcast-related freight off air transport by 2030, and logistics emissions are already down 29% versus the 2018 baseline. This simulator makes the remaining trade-offs tangible: set a freight strategy for each flyaway leg of the 2026 calendar and watch estimated CO₂, cost, and the operational consequences move.

1 · The simulator

Seventeen freight legs connect the 2026 flyaways (the European leg travels by biofuel truck and is out of scope here). Toggle each leg, or apply a strategy:

A red flag on a leg means the chosen mode is slower than the gap between races — possible only with a second, pre-positioned equipment set at the destination. That is not a bug: circulating duplicate sea sets is precisely how F1's real modal shift works. The flags count its capital cost.

All-sea cuts emissions two-thirds on paper but needs duplicate equipment on almost every tight leg — which is why the credible pathway mixes sea at season boundaries, hubs in the regional clusters, and air only where the calendar forces it.

Method & sourcing
Sources & method: F1 published sustainability reporting (2018 baseline; −29% logistics; hub and sea-freight strategy; biofuel truck fleet in Europe), the 2026 race calendar, public freight-emissions conversion factors (GLEC/DEFRA ranges). All figures are clearly-labeled estimates — real freight costs and tonnages are confidential. Data shared with the Net Zero Progress Tracker.
