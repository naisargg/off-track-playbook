Merchandise & Collab Strategy Teardown — Ferrari vs. the grid

Ferrari is the only team on the grid whose licensing arm — fashion line, theme parks, luxury goods — is a genuine standalone business. This teardown maps that portfolio against rivals' collab strategies (Cadillac × Tommy Hilfiger, Williams × UNDONE, McLaren's apparel deals) and asks what each is actually optimizing for.

1 · The positioning map

Every team monetizes fandom; almost none of them are playing the same game. Two axes separate the strategies: how far the licensing portfolio reaches beyond race-day merchandise, and where it sits on price.

Qualitative audit positioning from public portfolios — anchors: Ferrari's fashion, theme-park and luxury lines; Cadillac × Tommy Hilfiger; Williams × UNDONE; McLaren's apparel deals; ~$140M of Puma/Adidas sports-apparel money entering the grid in two years.

The read: Ferrari sits alone in the top-right — and is exposed by it. A luxury-priced portfolio meets a younger, more female, more price-sensitive fanbase. The white space isn't more luxury; it's the bridge.

2 · Three collab proposals for the new fanbase

47% of new US fans are 18–24 and over half are female. Each proposal attacks that audience from a different angle while protecting the luxury core:

Proposal 01 · Apparel bridgeCapsule with a female-led streetwear label

Limited co-branded drops timed to Miami and Las Vegas — the two races where the new audience is physically present. Price band $60–150: above commodity merch, far below the fashion line, no cannibalization.

Why it works: scarcity mechanics match how this audience already buys; the partner's credibility imports authenticity money can't.

KPIs: sell-through rate, % first-time Ferrari customers, social reach per unit produced.

Proposal 02 · Story-led lineF1 Academy graduate collection

A collection built on the Academy story — the pathway, the drivers, the colours — with a visible share of proceeds funding karting scholarships for girls.

Why it works: the female fanbase is the fastest-growing segment and this is the only merch story that is genuinely about them rather than resized for them.

KPIs: female share of buyers, media value of the launch story, scholarship applications generated.

Proposal 03 · Retention enginePassaporto — the 24-race collection game

A digital passport in the team app: each race weekend unlocks a stamp, trackside pop-ups carry exclusive physical counterparts, completed regions unlock rewards.

Why it works: converts 24 disconnected pop-up visits into one season-long collection loop — and hands the team first-party purchase data across 21 countries.

KPIs: repeat-purchase rate, app-to-till conversion, CRM records per race.

3 · Demand forecasting for 24 pop-ups

Trackside retail is 24 one-weekend stores in 21 countries — an inventory problem disguised as a merchandising one. A simple archetype model beats gut-feel allocation:

Race archetypeDemand characterStock skew
Destination spectacle — Vegas, Miami, SingaporeHigh casual share, first-time buyers, giftingEntry price points, capsule drops, women's cuts deep
Heartland classic — Monza, Silverstone, ZandvoortCommitted fans, repeat buyers, team loyaltyCore replica range, premium collector items
New market — Madrid debutUnknown baseline, high varianceConservative depth, fast mid-weekend replenishment plan
Flyaway classic — Suzuka, São Paulo, MelbourneLocal hero effects, currency sensitivityLocalized pricing, driver-specific lines

Forecast = attendance × archetype conversion × basket, corrected by app pre-orders and social geo-split. Even a crude model turns markdowns and stockouts — the two ways pop-ups lose money — into a managed variable.
